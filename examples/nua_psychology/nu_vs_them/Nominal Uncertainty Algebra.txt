Abstract

Psychological science routinely aggregates information, rescales variables, builds composite indices, and pools evidence across studies. Yet the discipline still lacks a standardized, assumption‑light method for tracking how uncertainty moves through the arithmetic steps that underlie nearly every analysis: adding and subtracting means, multiplying by scalars to rescale, forming indices and weighted composites, and aggregating across studies. Existing approaches either (a) assume distributional forms and rely on variance algebra (e.g., Gaussian error propagation; inverse‑variance meta‑analysis), or (b) rely on model‑based inferential machinery that makes uncertainty accounting sensitive to prior specification and publication bias.

1. Introduction: The need for robust uncertainty propagation

Across subfields, psychology’s empirical claims depend on arithmetic: researchers sum item scores into scales, rescale outcomes, compute contrasts, and pool effect sizes across studies. The epistemic status of these operations rests on how error is propagated, yet standard practice either presumes Gaussian errors (so that variances add and scale in familiar ways) or relies on model‑based pooling where heterogeneity and small‑study effects can dominate conclusions.

+2
 Contemporary meta‑science has made the stakes clear: replication audit projects and many‑analysts demonstrations show that reasonable analysis choices can yield materially different conclusions from the same data, which implies that uncertainty lives inside our arithmetic as much as inside our models.

N/U algebra addresses this gap by providing a deliberately conservative, assumption‑light ledger that tracks numerical values alongside honest uncertainty budgets at each arithmetic step—without committing to specific distributional forms or hierarchical priors. The approach is aligned in spirit with long‑standing engineering standards (e.g., GUM) and interval‑analysis traditions that prefer transparent error bounds when assumptions are fragile.

 It is intended not to supplant frequentist or Bayesian inference, but to stand beside them as a robustness panel that is stable under model changes and publication‑bias diagnostics.

2. Current Practices in Psychology: How uncertainty is propagated
2.1 Frequentist machinery

In the frequentist paradigm, uncertainty is often treated via confidence intervals based on sampling distributions and—when combining evidence across studies—inverse‑variance weighted fixed‑ or random‑effects meta‑analysis. 
Cochrane
 The field’s canonical references outline heterogeneity quantification using Cochran’s Q, τ² (between‑study variance), and I² (proportion of variance due to heterogeneity), with best practices evolving toward prediction intervals that express dispersion in future settings.

4. Complementary Value: Using N/U as a robustness panel
4.1 Contrasts and rescaling

In ANOVA‑style contrasts and simple rescaling (e.g., converting milliseconds to standardized units), N/U’s linear transform rule tracks how each step affects the budget, creating an auditable ledger independent of distributional assumptions. This complements frequentist CIs (which assume a sampling model) and Bayesian posteriors (which assume priors) by exhibiting the arithmetic footprint of every transform. 
Cochrane
+1

4.2 Composite scores and weighted indices

Psychology often builds scales (sum scores) or weighted composites (e.g., factor‑score–like indices). An N/U ledger shows exactly how adding items or applying weights inflates or compresses budgets, guarding against illusory precision in long indices. When composites feed a meta‑analysis, the ledger interfaces naturally with traditional pooling by making the pre‑meta arithmetic explicit. 
Cochrane

5. Applications with Open Datasets

We sketch how an N/U robustness panel would sit alongside standard analyses:

Many Labs 2 (ML2). Protocol‑vetted, preregistered replications across 125 samples and >15,000 participants test generalizability of classic findings. For each effect, maintain an N/U ledger at the site level (contrast → rescaling → composite) and pool conservatively across sites next to random‑effects estimates and prediction intervals; divergence spotlights heterogeneity patterns. 
SAGE Journals

Open Anchoring Quest (OpAQ). Item‑level, multi‑study anchoring dataset. Build per‑item ledgers for anchored estimates before forming study‑level composites; then contrast N/U brackets with selection‑model–adjusted Bayesian meta‑analysis (RoBMA) to quantify sensitivity to publication bias. 
Journal of Open Psychology Data
+1

psymetadata. A corpus of psychological effect sizes and metadata. Use N/U to audit pre‑meta arithmetic (e.g., standardized mean differences assembled from raw means/SDs) before conventional pooling; differences between N/U envelopes and inverse‑variance CIs indicate where preprocessing, not pooling, drives uncertainty.

7. Limitations and scope

N/U is conservative by design; brackets may be wider than model‑based intervals. That is a feature when dependence is unknown or when multiverse choices multiply. Where measurement models are well‑validated and assumptions hold, traditional inferential machinery may provide tighter (but assumption‑dependent) intervals; the point of the companion panel is to expose that dependence. 
Cochrane
 N/U also does not replace construct validation or measurement theory; it complements them by making the arithmetic of scores and transforms auditable. 
BIPM

8. Conclusion

N/U algebra adds a missing piece to psychology’s evidence workflow: an assumption‑light, conservative, and transparent arithmetic ledger that travels with the data from raw numbers to inferential summaries, sits comfortably beside frequentist and Bayesian tools, and communicates where uncertainty actually enters our numbers.
N/U algebra is prepared to serve as a conservative companion method in psychology, ensuring transparency and robustness.
