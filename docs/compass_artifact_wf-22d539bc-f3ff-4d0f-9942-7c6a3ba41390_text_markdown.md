# Standards Organizations and Certification Pathways for N/U Algebra

N/U Algebra has strong alignment with multiple international standards organizations, with **IEEE and JCGM/BIPM offering the most direct pathways** based on existing precedents for mathematical frameworks handling conservative uncertainty propagation. The mathematical properties of N/U Algebra—closure, associativity, monotonicity, and guaranteed conservative bounds—position it well for standardization across metrology, numerical computing, and safety-critical systems domains. Multiple pathways exist requiring **3-7 year timelines** and **investment ranging from $25,000-$100,000** depending on chosen route.

## Most promising pathways across all standards bodies

The research reveals **three tier-one opportunities** for N/U Algebra standardization. First, IEEE standards development through the Computer Society offers the clearest precedent with IEEE 1788-2015 Interval Arithmetic demonstrating IEEE's receptivity to conservative uncertainty propagation frameworks. The process requires IEEE SA membership ($300-600/year), submission of a Project Authorization Request through the myProject system, and approximately 3-5 years of working group development. Second, JCGM Working Group 1 maintains the Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement (GUM) and actively develops supplements for new mathematical methods including Monte Carlo and Bayesian approaches. Third, ISO/IEC Technical Reports provide a faster initial standardization pathway (2-4 years) with potential evolution to full International Standards.

The conservative bounds property of N/U Algebra represents **a strategic advantage** rather than limitation across all domains. Safety-critical standards like DO-178C explicitly identify "inadequate numerical resolution" as a failure mode that N/U Algebra directly addresses. Functional safety standards IEC 61508 and ISO 26262 require demonstrable correctness under all operating conditions, which N/U Algebra's guaranteed bounds support. Academic recognition through SIAM's Activity Group on Uncertainty Quantification offers credibility without formal certification, leveraging peer-reviewed publication and community adoption.

## International metrology and uncertainty standards

### JCGM and the GUM framework: the natural home for uncertainty methods

The Joint Committee for Guides in Metrology chairs the global authority for measurement uncertainty standards, maintaining the Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement through its Working Group 1. **JCGM-WG1 has demonstrated receptivity to new mathematical methods** by publishing supplements on Monte Carlo propagation (JCGM 101:2008), multivariate models (JCGM 102:2011), and developing Bayesian methods (JCGM 108 in progress). The committee comprises representatives from eight international organizations—BIPM, IEC, IFCC, ILAC, ISO, IUPAC, IUPAP, and OIML—ensuring global reach and authority.

N/U Algebra aligns precisely with JCGM-WG1's mission of developing methods for "evaluation and expression of measurement uncertainty." The working group explicitly acknowledges situations where standard GUM approaches may be insufficient and conducts special meetings on "new measures of uncertainty." Recent publications including GUM-6:2020 on measurement models and GUM-1:2023 introductory guidance demonstrate active evolution of the framework.

**Submission requires indirect pathways** through member organizations since JCGM does not accept direct proposals from individuals. The most effective approach involves working through National Metrology Institutes (NMIs) like NIST in the USA, PTB in Germany, or NPL in the UK. Researchers should first publish in *Metrologia* (BIPM's official journal), present at BIPM-sponsored workshops, and develop worked examples from diverse measurement fields. When JCGM circulates draft documents for public comment, engaged stakeholders can provide input through any of the eight member organizations. **Building constituency across multiple member organizations** proves critical, as approval requires consensus among all eight international bodies.

The development timeline spans **5-10 years** from initial community engagement to published supplement. JCGM-WG1 meets 2-3 times annually with generous comment periods allowing thorough international review. No direct fees apply for participation through member organizations, though organizations bear costs of expert time and travel. The conservative approach favors supplements maintaining coherence with original GUM rather than wholesale revisions, positioning N/U Algebra ideally as "GUM Supplement 5: Conservative Uncertainty Propagation Using Nominal/Uncertainty Algebra."

### BIPM institutional structure and engagement pathways

The Bureau International des Poids et Mesures, established by the 1875 Metre Convention, provides the organizational foundation for international metrology. **The BIPM Director chairs JCGM** and the organization hosts JCGM secretariat services, making BIPM engagement essential for JCGM pathways. With headquarters at Pavillon de Breteuil in Sèvres, France, BIPM operates through scientific departments covering Chemistry and Biology, Ionizing Radiation, Physical Metrology, and Time.

Contact with BIPM occurs primarily through participation in Consultative Committees organized by measurement domain (CCAUV for acoustics, CCEM for electricity, CCL for length, CCM for mass, CCPR for photometry, CCQM for chemistry, CCRI for radiation, CCT for thermometry, CCTF for time, CCU for units). These committees coordinate with National Metrology Institutes that participate in BIPM work programs. Direct contact information includes +33 1 45 07 70 70 and webmaster@bipm.org, though substantive technical proposals should flow through NMI representatives.

### ISO/IEC uncertainty measurement standards and submission processes

ISO/IEC Guide 98-3:2008, published jointly with BIPM as the authoritative GUM, falls under ISO Technical Management Board Groups oversight. The document costs CHF 221 (~$250 USD) though HTML versions are freely available online. **ISO/IEC provides formal standards development** infrastructure complementing JCGM's scientific authority, with JCGM documents published as the ISO/IEC Guide 98 series.

For N/U Algebra standardization through ISO/IEC, three pathways exist. First, **Technical Reports (TR) offer the recommended starting path** for introducing N/U Algebra to the international community. TRs provide informational documentation without normative requirements, develop faster than full standards (18-36 months), and can evolve to full standards after field validation. The appropriate target would be ISO/IEC TR under JTC 1/SC 22 (Programming Languages, Mathematical Software Interfaces) with title "Information Technology — Mathematical methods for uncertainty quantification — N/U Algebra framework."

Second, Technical Specifications (TS) allow provisional standardization with 6-year lifecycles for market validation before full standard development. Third, full International Standards (IS) provide authoritative normative requirements but require proven implementation track records, broad stakeholder consensus, and **3-5 year development timelines** after New Work Item Proposal approval.

**All proposals must flow through national ISO member bodies** like ANSI (USA), BSI (UK), DIN (Germany), or AFNOR (France). The New Work Item Proposal (NWIP) process requires completion of ISO Form 4 detailing title and scope, justification and market relevance, stakeholder impact analysis, global relevance to sustainable development goals, review of existing work and duplication assessment, nominated project leader, preparatory work including outline or initial draft, resource commitments, and target development timeline. Voting requires 2/3 majority of Technical Committee P-members with at least 5 members committed to participate and less than 1/4 total votes negative.

Participation fees through ANSI cost **$575 per P-membership** annually, with organizations holding 10+ memberships eligible for negotiated rates. Development costs primarily reflect expert time commitments, travel to TC/SC meetings (approximately $5,000-15,000 annually for 2-4 meetings), and documentation preparation. Total estimated cost over a 3-year TR development cycle ranges **$25,000-50,000**.

## National standards organizations and government research institutes

### NIST divisions and programs for uncertainty quantification

The National Institute of Standards and Technology offers multiple engagement pathways with stronger emphasis on collaborative research than formal certification. **Two NIST divisions provide primary uncertainty quantification focus**: the Physical Measurement Laboratory (PML) maintaining comprehensive resources on measurement uncertainty following GUM, and the Information Technology Laboratory's Statistical Engineering Division (SED) specializing in measurement uncertainty theory, statistical methods for metrology, and calibration methods.

PML's Grant Program funds individual projects in the **$5,000-$250,000 per year range** with project performance periods up to 5 years, explicitly including measurement uncertainty characterization and statistical methods in metrology as focus areas. The NIST Uncertainty Machine at uncertainty.nist.gov provides web-based software for evaluating measurement uncertainty using Gauss and Monte Carlo methods, representing a potential integration target for N/U Algebra implementations.

NIST's Applied and Computational Mathematics Division (ACMD) develops mathematical models for scientific experiments and computational techniques for measurement science, maintaining the Digital Library of Mathematical Functions. The Uncertainty Quantification in Computational Materials Science program develops technical tools and builds communities of practice, having conducted workshops at Purdue University on UQ methods including Bayesian calibration.

**NIST Technical Note 1297** (1994 edition) by Taylor and Kuyatt provides foundational "Guidelines for Evaluating and Expressing the Uncertainty of NIST Measurement Results," while **NIST Technical Note 1900** (2015) by Possolo offers a "Simple Guide for Evaluating and Expressing the Uncertainty of NIST Measurement Results" that widens the meaning of measurement, characterizes uncertainty probabilistically, and describes the NIST Uncertainty Machine, bootstrap methods, and Monte Carlo approaches.

### Collaborative research pathways with NIST

Cooperative Research and Development Agreements (CRADAs) provide formal legal frameworks for research with predefined objectives where both NIST and partners contribute personnel, facilities, and equipment. **NIST cannot provide funds to partners**, but partners receive exclusive options to license NIST inventions developed during collaboration, access to unique reagents and resources, and confidentiality of research results for up to 5 years. Products must be substantially manufactured in the U.S. for licensing eligibility. Research Collaboration Agreements (RCAs) permit two-way exchange of materials and information with less formal structure than CRADAs.

Contact occurs through the **Technology Partnerships Office at TPO@nist.gov**, which negotiates agreements, manages NIST's intellectual property portfolio, and provides pathways from discovery to commercialization. The process involves contacting TPO to discuss goals and conduct needs assessment, connection to dedicated partnerships officer, determination of appropriate agreement type, and formal collaboration establishment.

For N/U Algebra, the recommended approach targets **ITL Statistical Engineering Division or PML divisions** working on uncertainty. Researchers should develop specific research objectives demonstrating N/U Algebra applications to NIST measurement challenges, propose computational implementations, and demonstrate improvements over existing uncertainty quantification methods. Timeline spans 6-12 months for CRADA establishment followed by 2-5 years collaboration with potential outcomes including NIST publications, validation studies, and possible adoption in NIST tools.

### ANSI standards development and accreditation

The American National Standards Institute facilitates and coordinates U.S. voluntary standards serving as U.S. representative to ISO and founding member of the International Accreditation Forum. ANSI oversees approximately 235 Accredited Standards Developers (ASDs) that develop American National Standards (ANS) carrying federal recognition under OMB Circular A-119, National Technology Transfer and Advancement Act, and WTO Technical Barriers to Trade Code of Good Practice.

**Two pathways exist**: working with existing ASDs or becoming a new ASD. Existing ASDs working in related areas include ASTM International (materials testing, statistical methods), IEEE (computational methods), and ASME (engineering standards). Researchers can contact these organizations to propose new standards or modifications to existing standards and participate in technical committees.

Organizations seeking ASD accreditation submit applications demonstrating procedures meeting ANSI's due process requirements including openness, balance, lack of dominance, due process, and consensus. **Application fees cost $5,000 one-time** with annual maintenance fees based on organization size. The two-phase standards development process includes a Proposed Standard Phase (initial proposal addressing conflict/duplication claims) and Draft Standard Phase (voting and public comment with minimum 60 days). Standards development timelines range 6 months to several years depending on complexity and consensus achievement.

## IEEE standards for interval arithmetic and numerical methods

### IEEE 1788-2015 interval arithmetic: the closest precedent

IEEE Standard 1788-2015 for Interval Arithmetic, approved June 30, 2015 and currently active within its 10-year lifecycle, **provides the most relevant precedent** for N/U Algebra standardization. The standard specifies basic interval arithmetic operations for conservative uncertainty propagation using mathematical interval models, supports IEEE 754 floating-point formats, defines exception conditions and standard handling, and structures implementation from mathematical model to computer levels. IEEE 1788.1-2017 offers a simplified subset focusing on commonly used operations with binary64 floating-point numbers.

Working Group P1788 completed the standard after 7-year development (2008-2015) with mailing list STDS-1788@LISTSERV.IEEE.ORG and website https://grouper.ieee.org/groups/1788/. **The interval arithmetic precedent demonstrates IEEE's acceptance** of conservative uncertainty propagation frameworks using ordered pairs and mathematical closure properties, providing strong alignment with N/U Algebra's approach.

N/U Algebra could pursue either a new standard titled "IEEE Standard for Nominal/Uncertainty Algebra" or an amendment adding N/U Algebra as alternative "flavor" within the IEEE 1788 framework. The amendment pathway offers faster development but may limit scope, requiring engagement with the P1788 community. A broader working group on "Uncertainty Quantification Methods" could develop families of related standards encompassing N/U Algebra and complementary frameworks.

### IEEE standards development process

IEEE standards development follows **six basic steps** requiring 3-6 years total timeline. The Project Authorization Request (PAR) must be submitted by IEEE SA members through myProject online system at https://development.standards.ieee.org/myproject with oversight from IEEE SA Standards Board-approved Standards Committees. PAR components include project title and scope, purpose and need for standard, stakeholders affected, expected completion timeline (maximum 4 years from PAR submission), balloting group type (individual or entity-based), and Working Group Chair contact information (must be IEEE SA member).

New Standards Committee (NesCom) reviews PARs quarterly with either regular processing (up to 90 days) or continuous processing (25 days maximum via teleconference). After PAR approval, working groups mobilize with no IEEE membership required for participation in individual-based projects, though all meetings must be open to interested parties. **Working groups strive for broad, balanced representation** with typical development periods of 2-4 years.

Drafting follows the IEEE Standards Style Manual through working group consensus with technical editors compiling contributions. Balloting requires **75% return rate and 75% approval rate** with ballot group members being IEEE SA members or paying per-ballot fees. Comments must be addressed, especially DISAPPROVE comments, with recirculation ballots potentially required for significant disapprovals. Final approval flows through IEEE SA Standards Board Review Committee (RevCom) reviewing against bylaws and operations manual, followed by Standards Board majority vote.

Maintenance occurs through 10-year validity periods with amendments and corrigenda developable without affecting validity. Standards must be reviewed for revision, reaffirmation, or withdrawal at 10-year marks. **IEEE 1788 took 7 years** from initiation to publication, though timelines vary based on complexity, working group activity, ballot recirculations needed, and comment resolution efficiency.

### IEEE membership and cost structure

**IEEE SA Individual Membership** enables unlimited balloting on individual-based projects, eligibility for Working Group Chair positions, voting rights for IEEE SA governance, and ability to lead working groups. Members must first be IEEE members or join simultaneously, with pricing varying by country and career phase. IEEE SA membership adds reduced cost to IEEE membership, proving more cost-effective than per-ballot fees for multiple ballots.

PAR submission carries no direct fee but requires IEEE SA membership. Working group participation in individual-based projects is **free for all participants** without IEEE membership required. Balloting offers two options: IEEE SA membership (unlimited balloting) or per-ballot fee comparable to annual IEEE SA membership covering one ballot group and recirculations, arranged 5+ working days before invitation closing and not processable online.

**Total minimum annual cost** approximates $300-600/year for key participants (IEEE membership ~$200-400 plus IEEE SA membership ~$100-200). Additional costs include optional travel to working group meetings (can meet virtually), conference participation for community building, technical documentation development (time investment), and implementation development for proof of concept. **The primary investment is time** rather than money, with working groups typically requiring 2-4 years of active development with regular meetings and significant volunteer effort.

### Recommended IEEE pathway for N/U Algebra

**Phase 1 Foundation (6-12 months)** involves joining IEEE and IEEE SA as individual member, forming informal study group of interested parties, presenting N/U Algebra at IEEE conferences, publishing in IEEE journals/transactions, documenting use cases and implementations, and contacting IEEE Computer Society Standards Activities Board Chair. **Phase 2 Formal Initiation (3-6 months)** identifies sponsoring Standards Committees, develops PAR with study group input, submits through myProject system, and awaits NesCom review and approval.

**Phase 3 Standards Development (2-4 years)** forms and mobilizes working groups, drafts standards following IEEE Style Manual, conducts regular meetings, builds consensus on technical content, and prepares for balloting. **Phase 4 Approval (6-12 months)** forms ballot groups, conducts SA ballots, addresses comments with recirculation if needed, submits to RevCom, and obtains Standards Board approval. The **expected total timeline spans 3-6 years** from initial concept to published standard.

N/U Algebra represents a strong candidate for IEEE standardization given clear technical merit, practical applications, and alignment with IEEE's mission. The existence of IEEE 1788-2015 as closely related standard demonstrates IEEE's receptivity to conservative uncertainty propagation frameworks. Success requires sustained community engagement, technical rigor, and commitment to consensus-based standards development processes.

## Safety-critical systems and functional safety standards

### Aerospace standards: RTCA, EUROCAE, and DO-178C numerical requirements

RTCA (Radio Technical Commission for Aeronautics) develops standards serving as basis for FAA certification as private non-profit corporation working with FAA and international partners. **DO-178C Software Considerations in Airborne Systems and Equipment Certification**, published jointly with EUROCAE as DO-178C/ED-12C in December 2011, provides primary document for FAA/EASA software certification approval defining Design Assurance Levels (DAL A-E) based on failure severity.

**The standard explicitly addresses numerical precision**, identifying as software errors "inadequate end-to-end numerical resolution," "inadequate algorithm precision, accuracy or performance," "failure of an algorithm to satisfy a software requirement," and "incorrect computation sequence." DO-178C supplemental standards include DO-331 (Model-Based Development & Verification), DO-332 (Object-Oriented Technology), DO-333 (Formal Methods), and DO-330 (Software Tool Qualification).

DO-254 Design Assurance Guidance for Airborne Electronic Hardware, recognized by FAA in 2005 and updated to AMC 20-152A, applies to complex electronic hardware including FPGAs, ASICs, and PLDs. Requirements-based design and verification approaches mandate traceability throughout hardware development with **hardware requiring demonstrable correctness under all operating conditions** including numerical accuracy. This requirement aligns perfectly with N/U Algebra's conservative bounds guaranteeing correct performance ranges.

RTCA Special Committee formation requires FAA requests or 2+ RTCA member company petitions to Program Management Committee (PMC) with Terms of Reference defining scope, need, boundaries, and intended use. Committee leadership requires member company sponsorship and leadership commitment with typical **2-year timelines for initial document development**. Final Review and Comment (FRAC) includes public consultation with minimum 45 days followed by PMC approval and publication authorization.

### EUROCAE European aviation standards development

The European Organisation for Civil Aviation Equipment, founded 1963 and based in Paris with 500+ worldwide members, serves as European leader in aviation standards development with 5,000+ experts across 50+ Working Groups. EUROCAE jointly publishes DO-178C/ED-12C, DO-254/ED-80, and DO-160/ED-14 with RTCA, organizing through 11 technical domains including ATM, Airport, Avionics, Cabin, and System Engineering.

Working Group participation flows from public calls for experts with organizations nominating participants and EUROCAE membership recommended but not mandatory for participation. Technical Work Programme strategic documents outline scope with open consultation on draft standards and final Council approval. The organization coordinates through European ATM Standards Coordination Group, European UAS Standards Coordination Group, and European Cyber Security Coordination Group preventing overlap and ensuring comprehensive coverage.

For N/U Algebra engagement, target opportunities include **SC-240 Topics on Software Advancement** and **SC-243/WG-128 Airborne Electronic Hardware** with DO-254 Revision A planned. Researchers should contact RTCA membership office exploring participation opportunities, prepare technical papers on N/U Algebra for numerical verification, and attend RTCA/EUROCAE public meetings as observers initially.

### IEC 61508 functional safety and SIL levels

IEC 61508 Functional Safety of Electrical/Electronic/Programmable Electronic Safety-Related Systems, published 1998-2000 (first edition) and revised 2010 (second edition), serves as **universal functional safety standard** applying to all industries with seven-part structure covering complete safety lifecycle and defining Safety Integrity Levels (SIL 1-4). The standard addresses safety lifecycle from concept through decommissioning, probabilistic risk assessment for safety functions, systematic failure prevention through development processes, random hardware failure detection and control, and **uncertainty management** in safety-related systems.

Industry-specific derivatives include IEC 62061 (Machinery), IEC 61511 (Process Industry), IEC 61513 (Nuclear Power Plants), IEC 62278 (Railway Applications), and ISO 26262 (Automotive). Standards development follows New Work Item Proposal through National Committees, Working Group formation by Technical Committees or Sub-Committees, Committee Draft circulation, Committee Draft for Vote international circulation, Final Draft International Standard voting, and publication of approved standards.

Submission routes flow **through National Committees** like ANSI for USA requiring demonstrated industry need, expert participation, and global relevance with consensus potential. For N/U Algebra, positioning as complement to IEC 61508 Part 3 (Software) targeting numerical methods for safety-related systems offers potential pathway, though timeline spans 3-5 years typical for international standards.

### ISO 26262 automotive functional safety

ISO 26262 Automotive Functional Safety, published 2011 (first edition) and revised 2018 (second edition), adapts IEC 61508 for automotive E/E systems through 12-part structure (10 normative, 2 guidelines) applying to road vehicles up to 3,500 kg. **Automotive Safety Integrity Levels** (ASIL) range from QM (Quality Management without special safety requirements) through ASIL A (least stringent), B, C, to D (most stringent for catastrophic hazards) based on severity of harm, probability of exposure, and controllability by driver.

The standard requires complete safety lifecycle management, hazard analysis and risk assessment, safety goals with integrity levels, hardware and software development processes, verification and validation activities, and addressing both systematic failures (design errors) and random hardware failures. **Certification authorities treat ISO 26262 as technical state of the art** with legal liability implications, requiring demonstrable safety under all operating conditions.

N/U Algebra's conservative bounds in numerical methods provide safety margins directly supporting ISO 26262 requirements as automotive systems increasingly rely on numerical calculations in throttle-by-wire, Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS), and autonomous vehicle perception. Conservative bounds provide objective safety evidence simplifying certification arguments through deterministic guarantees without statistical assumptions.

### ASME verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification standards

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers publishes **ASME PTC 19.1-2018 Test Uncertainty** as fundamental standard for measurement uncertainty in test results, specifying procedures for evaluation of uncertainties in measurements, propagation into test results, Type A (statistical) and Type B (other means) classification, and confidence level determination. The standard enjoys wide adoption as national/international standard methodology with emphasis on conservative uncertainty bounds for safety-critical applications.

ASME VVUQ Standards include VVUQ 1-2022 (Terminology in Computational Modeling and Simulation), V&V 10-2019 (Verification and Validation in Computational Solid Mechanics), V&V 20-2009 (V&V in CFD and Heat Transfer), V&V 40-2018 (Assessing Credibility for Medical Devices), and VVUQ 90 (Computational Modeling of Airframe Structures). Key concepts distinguish **verification** (does computational model fit mathematical description?), **validation** (does model accurately represent real-world application?), and **uncertainty quantification** (how do parameter variations affect outcomes?).

**Free membership in VVUQ subcommittees** requires technical expertise and active participation with contact through Daniel Papert (212.591.7000) or Lydia Stanford (212.591.8031). The annual VVUQ Symposium features technical sessions on uncertainty quantification methods, challenge problems to assess VVUQ approaches, and networking opportunities with standards community. N/U Algebra's guaranteed conservative bounds align perfectly with ASME philosophy preferring conservative approaches for safety-critical applications.
