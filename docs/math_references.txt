Peer-Reviewed Sources Supporting “After-the-Fact Mathematics”
Imre Lakatos – Proofs and Refutations
Source: Lakatos, I. (1976). Proofs and Refutations: The Logic of Mathematical Discovery. Cambridge University Press.

Originally serialized in the British Journal for the Philosophy of Science (1963–64).

📌 Core support for quasi-empirical, temporally ordered math discovery.

Imre Lakatos – Quasi-Empiricism
Source: Lakatos, I. (1978). “Mathematics, Science and Epistemology,” in Philosophical Papers, Volume 2, Cambridge University Press.

Includes: A Renaissance of Empiricism in the Recent Philosophy of Mathematics.

Argues math proceeds through conjecture, refutation, correction—not deductive finality.

Philip Kitcher – Empirical View of Mathematics
Source: Kitcher, P. (1983). The Nature of Mathematical Knowledge. Oxford University Press.

Asserts math knowledge is synthetic and historical, built from practice—not Platonic truth.

Peer-reviewed analysis:

Dorato, M. (2018). “Kitcher, Mathematics, and Apriority.” Erkenntnis, 83(4), 683–705.
Springer Link

Penelope Maddy – Mathematical Naturalism
Source: Maddy, P. (1997). Naturalism in Mathematics. Oxford University Press.

Argues that math is a natural human practice, not abstract a priori discovery.

Charlotte Werndl – Definitions Beyond Lakatos
Source: Werndl, C. (2014). “Justifying Definitions in Mathematics—Going Beyond Lakatos.” Philosophia Mathematica, 22(3), 281–305.

Preprint on arXiv

Shows that definitions in modern math (e.g., ergodic theory) arise from historical and practical constraints—not purely logical necessity.

Andrew Aberdein – Proof vs Evidence Distinction
Source: Aberdein, A. (2019). “Evidence, Proofs, and Derivations.” Philosophia Mathematica, 27(3), 314–339.

Preprint

Clarifies how "proof" often obscures the historical and evidential context of mathematical work.

Historical Case Studies – Euler & Non-Euclidean Geometry
These are embedded in the above works but also discussed extensively in:

Lakatos (1976) Proofs and Refutations – Euler's polyhedron formula as central example.

Gray, J. (1989). Ideas of Space: Euclidean, Non-Euclidean, and Relativistic. Oxford University Press.

Shows how non-Euclidean geometry emerged only after failed attempts at deductive closure.

Greenberg, M. J. (2008). Euclidean and Non-Euclidean Geometries: Development and History. W. H. Freeman.

Emphasizes how “definition” lagged behind discovery in geometrical models.
